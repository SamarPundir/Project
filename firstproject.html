Chrysler Mopar 914HR Transistor Radio

Chrysler and Philco announced that they had developed and produced the world's first all-transistor car radio in the April 28th 1955 edition of the Wall Street Journal.[15] Chrysler made the all-transistor car radio, Mopar model 914HR, available as an "option" in fall 1955 for its new line of 1956 Chrysler and Imperial cars, which hit the showroom floor on October 21, 1955. The all-transistor car radio was a $150 option (equivalent to $1,640 in 2022).

Features:

  • This Transisto radio often featured knobs or sliders for adjusting the volume and tone (bass and treble) settings. Users could customize the audio output based on their preferences.
  • This Transistor radio were equipped with a built-in carrying handle, enhancing their portability. This model also had a leather or plastic case for protection during transport.
  • The controls on transistor radio were generally simple and straightforward, making them user-friendly. This simplicity contributed to the widespread adoption of these radios.

Raytheon 8-TO-1 Transistor Radio

In February 1955, the second transistor radio, the 8-TP-1, was introduced by Raytheon. It was a larger portable transistor radio, including an expansive four-inch speaker and four additional transistors (the TR-1 used only four). As a result, the sound quality was much better than the TR-1. An additional benefit of the 8-TP-1 was its efficient battery consumption. In July 1955, the first positive review of a transistor radio appeared in the Consumer Reports that said, "The transistors in this set have not been used in an effort to build the smallest radio on the market, and good performance has not been sacrificed."

Features:

  • This Radio has a tuning dial that allowed users to select different radio frequencies. This was often a manual dial that users would turn to tune in to their desired station.
  • This Transistor Radio usually had a built-in telescopic antenna or a simple wire antenna. Extending the antenna could improve reception and help capture radio signals more effectively.
  • This radio typically had a built-in speaker for audio output. The size and quality of the speaker varied between models, affecting the sound quality.

Regency TR-1 Transistor Radio

Two companies working together, Texas Instruments of Dallas, and Industrial Development Engineering Associates (I.D.E.A.) of Indianapolis, Indiana, were behind the unveiling of the Regency TR-1, the world's first commercially produced transistor radio. Previously, Texas Instruments was producing instrumentation for the oil industry and locating devices for the U.S. Navy and I.D.E.A. built home television antenna boosters. The two companies worked together on the TR-1, looking to grow revenues for their respective companies by breaking into this new product area.[5] In May 1954, Texas Instruments had designed and built a prototype and was looking for an established radio manufacturer to develop and market a radio using their transistors.

Features:

  • The primary distinguishing feature was the use of transistors for amplification and signal processing instead of vacuum tubes. This led to smaller, lighter, and more energy-efficient radios.
  • One of the most significant advantages of transistor radios was their portability. They were compact and often designed to be carried easily, making them suitable for use on the go.
  • Transistor radios were typically powered by batteries, which contributed to their portability. The use of batteries eliminated the need for a direct electrical connection, allowing users to take their radios anywhere.
